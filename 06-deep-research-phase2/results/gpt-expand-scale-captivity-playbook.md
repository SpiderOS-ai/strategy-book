# Reverse-engineering Palantir’s post-pilot expand and scale playbook

## What can be proven from public evidence

Palantir’s own filings explicitly describe a three-stage account lifecycle: **Acquire, Expand, Scale**. In **Acquire**, Palantir says it “typically” wins new opportunities via **short-term pilot deployments** offered at **no or low cost** to the customer, and that it operates these accounts at a loss because it is “proving the value” of the platforms. citeturn16view3turn17view2

In Palantir’s definition, **Expand** is not a calendar stage or a pricing tier: it is an account state where annual recognised revenue is above a threshold, but contribution margin is still negative because Palantir is investing heavily to deepen and broaden the deployment. Palantir defines “Expand phase” customers as those with **more than $100,000 in recognised revenue** in a calendar year **and negative contribution margin** for that year (as determined at year end). citeturn16view3turn16view1

Similarly, **Scale** is not simply “big contract”: it is the point where Palantir classifies an account as having **more than $100,000 in recognised revenue** and a **positive contribution margin** for the year. Palantir states that contribution margin generally improves in the Scale phase, and gives an explicit example: in 2019 it generated **$565.7m** of revenue from Scale phase customers with **55%** contribution margin, and in H1 2020 those same customers generated **$296.3m** at **68%** contribution margin. citeturn16view0turn16view1

This matters for your “$100K pilot to $5.6m platform” framing. Palantir’s “$100K” is a **classification threshold** for phases (recognised revenue), not a universal pilot price; and the “$5.6m” figure that often gets repeated is, in the S-1, the **aggregate revenue** from customers acquired during **H1 2020**, not a single pilot conversion. citeturn16view3

The second pillar of publicly visible methodology is Palantir’s customer-side operating model, documented as **Foundry adoption phases**. These phases describe how the *customer’s internal programme* evolves: from a small number of use cases (Phase 1) to formal infrastructure and prioritisation mechanisms (Phase 2), to “platform growth through autonomy” with hundreds of users and formal governance (Phase 3), to “hypergrowth” where the programme is largely self-sufficient and Palantir engineers are used mainly for boosts and advisory. citeturn18view2turn18view3turn18view4

Your “captive and autonomous” question is therefore best answered as: **Operational autonomy inside the customer** (customer builds and runs most things) combined with **strategic captivity via embeddedness and switching costs**. Importantly, Palantir’s filings make clear that captivity is not contractual by default: customers may have no obligation to renew; contracts can be as short as one year; many contracts allow termination with notice, commonly three to six months; and customers may seek renegotiation. citeturn17view3

## The expand phase playbook in practice

**Expansion trigger identification: what FDEs are actually there to do.** Palantir states that customers’ decisions to expand depend partly on “the ability of our forward-deployed engineers to assist our customers in identifying new use cases [and] modernizing their data architectures”. citeturn17view0 Palantir also frames forward-deployed engineers as “on the front lines”, observing user challenges firsthand and feeding those learnings into continuous platform improvement. citeturn17view0

**The operational mechanism: a governed, always-on use case funnel.** In the Foundry adoption guidance, Phase 2 explicitly calls for “formal prioritization mechanisms” and a “formal roadmap” for expanding Foundry across business domains. It also specifies inbound and outbound programme management, including managing inbound development requests plus approval criteria and prioritisation mechanisms for new use cases. citeturn18view2

**What a “use case backlog” looks like when it is formal.** Palantir’s governance processes documentation describes a “Development and roadmap review” meeting, owned by the Programme Manager, held **monthly**, intended to align deliverables, discuss enhancements to existing use cases, and “identify and prioritize opportunities for new use cases”. It explicitly includes “assessment of business cases to determine and confirm use case value” and “making decisions based on the priority of deliverables”. citeturn18view0 At the executive layer, Palantir suggests a **quarterly SteerCo** to evaluate strategic direction, platform scope and cost, including review of live, in-development, and prospective use cases and associated costs and value. citeturn18view0

**How this becomes systematic rather than heroic.** The same governance guidance describes a “Project creation review” (monthly) where users submit new project requests through a programme-built request portal and those requests are approved or denied in an inbox workflow; it also emphasises controlling role assignment and access through a central governance team. citeturn18view0 This is the “scalable intake” mechanism that turns scattered ideas into a controlled expansion pipeline.

**AIP bootcamps as an expansion accelerant for existing customers.** Palantir positions AIP Bootcamps as a five-day, hands-on workshop for **new and existing customers** to go “from zero to use case”, and explicitly includes “onboard and train users for rollout in operations” as a bootcamp outcome. citeturn6search1turn9view0 In Palantir’s own write-up, the bootcamp is designed to both deliver a real use case and build the “intuition and skills to build independently moving forward”. citeturn9view0

**Why bootcamps create expansion momentum.** Palantir’s bootcamp rationale is not “demo theatre”: it is about compressing architecture, data, and feedback-loop decisions into a short cycle. The same source emphasises moving “from chat to automation” by grounding prompts in real-world events translated through the Ontology, and building “expert-driven feedback loops” that turn experience into durable advantage. citeturn9view0 This is exactly the sort of mechanism that, post-pilot, produces a backlog of further department-level opportunities.

**The “identify 100 use cases” claim: what can and cannot be proven.** Public Palantir materials do not describe a single canonical facilitation method (for example specific post-it formats) for generating “100 use cases” in a session. What is visible is that Palantir pushes customers toward an explicit scoping and prioritisation discipline. Palantir’s own learning catalogue includes a course on “Scoping use cases for Foundry & AIP” focused on prioritisation and the questions needed to scope a use case with stakeholders. citeturn0search32 It is reasonable to infer that “100 use cases” comes from a structured ideation funnel whose output is then triaged through monthly roadmap review and quarterly SteerCo, rather than the raw brainstorm itself being the methodology.

**How Palantir decides which department to expand into next: the evidence-backed levers.** The Foundry governance cadence strongly implies that expansion sequencing is decided by: business case value, cost, platform scope, progress, and user-base evolution, because those are the explicit SteerCo and roadmap-review agenda elements. citeturn18view0 Phase 2 also explicitly targets scaling across business domains and building programme infrastructure to manage growth. citeturn18view2

## Ontology expansion across departments

**What the Ontology is in Palantir terms: the expansion substrate.** Palantir describes the Foundry Ontology as an “operational layer” sitting on top of integrated digital assets (datasets, models) and connecting them to real-world entities and events, with governance and granular security. citeturn1search2turn0search1 In its Architecture Center, Palantir positions the Ontology as “the system at the heart” of the architecture, designed to represent interconnected enterprise decisions, enabling humans and AI agents to collaborate across workflows. citeturn0search5

**How it scales from one department to many: organisationally.** In Phase 2, Palantir recommends investing in building an “organizational ontology” and developing a team of Ontology Managers who can gather requirements, understand data availability, and contribute to ontological design. citeturn18view2 In Phase 3, Palantir expects “significant expansion of use cases” across business domains, with programme teams in place for feedback loops that ingest new requirements and projects into the roadmap, alongside data integrations and ontology maintenance. citeturn18view3

**Where governance actually lives: Ontology backlog plus approvals.** In the Phase 2 role definition, Palantir explicitly states that Ontology Managers “Manage the Ontology backlog, prioritizing change requests”, and “Evaluate all requests for new objects and other changes to the Ontology” with a view to broader impact and long-term evolution. citeturn20view0turn22search2 This is the closest thing to a formal “ontology governance board” in the public documentation: the Ontology is governed through a backlog managed by designated roles, wired into programme roadmap processes.

**Who approves new Object Types: governance as workflow, not committee theatre.** Palantir’s **Approvals** application manages “requesting, approving, and invoking” changes. It explicitly lists “Review ontology proposals” as an example workflow and notes that approvals persist as an audit log of past decisions. citeturn22search0 In other words, Ontology growth is meant to be auditable and routable, not purely informal.

**How the Ontology handles conflicting definitions and security boundaries: the shape of the solution.** Public documentation shows the building blocks Palantir expects you to use:
- The Ontology itself is designed with granular governance and security primitives. citeturn0search1turn18view3  
- Role design in Phase 3 includes a Permissions Manager responsible for group structures, SAML/identity integrations, and, at scale, restricted views and permission structures in collaboration with compliance. citeturn19view0  
- Palantir documents restricted views and markings as first-class governance constructs, with restricted views being integrated with Approvals workflows. citeturn22search6turn19view0  

**“Same customer exists in Sales CRM and Finance ERP with different IDs”: what Palantir pushes.** Palantir sells explicit **Entity Resolution** capabilities, describing “continuous matching of millions of records from disconnected systems” with end-to-end visibility into the resolution process, using hashing methods and AI/ML models. citeturn5search3 At the practitioner level, Palantir also publishes fuzzy-matching code examples (for example, name matching using similarity metrics) to support deduplication workflows. citeturn5search22

**The technical implication: Ontology growth is constrained by identity quality.** If you expand into new departments without resolving identity, you create a brittle Ontology that cannot safely support cross-domain workflows. The presence of a dedicated Entity Resolution product line, plus Phase-2 emphasis on centralised data-source engineering and Ontology backlog management, strongly suggests Palantir expects identity resolution to be treated as a programme capability, not a one-off fix. citeturn5search3turn20view0turn18view2

## Centre of excellence and champion system

**When a CoE forms: Palantir’s explicit signal.** In Palantir’s own Foundry adoption view, Phase 3 is where organisations “generally have hundreds of regular users”, governance is formalised, platform maintenance ownership is explicit, and training programmes run with clear owners. citeturn18view3 In that same Phase 3, Palantir introduces the **Center of Excellence** and states it should be a **rotation rather than a dedicated team**, used to fill skill gaps and boost initiatives without owning long-term domain accountability. citeturn19view0 This implies CoE formation is tied to crossing the “multi-use-case, multi-domain, hundreds-of-users” threshold more than to a specific month count.

**Who initiates: the programme design heavily implies customer leadership, with Palantir scaffolding.** Palantir’s Foundry Programme team is described as a centralised set of stakeholders “accountable for the Foundry program’s success”, like a “startup within your organization”. citeturn18view1 That language makes the initiation logic clear: the customer must own the programme, even if Palantir catalyses it.

**What the training curriculum looks like by role: evidence from role definitions and learning infrastructure.** Palantir’s adoption docs are unusually concrete about skill requirements and the specific Foundry applications each role should use, which functions as an implicit curriculum:
- The **Head of Platform** owns security, permissions administration, governance, compliance, performance, and cost allocation; and is expected to build documentation and trainings on best practices and compliance protocols. citeturn19view0  
- The **Permissions Manager** owns group structures, identity integration (including SAML) and, at scale, restricted views and markings; and must understand governance requirements and technical implementation. citeturn19view0turn22search6  
- **Agent Administrators** (your “data connectivity south team”) own data connection agents and are expected to have data engineering skills (SQL, Python, Spark) and close collaboration with data owners. citeturn19view0  
- **Data Source Developers** (also “south team”) are recommended as a centralised engineering capability owning ingestion, pipeline health, escalation SOPs and shared libraries for cleaning and obfuscation. citeturn20view0  
- **Ontology Managers** (your “ontology stewards”) explicitly own the Ontology backlog, evaluate requests for new objects, and align Ontology evolution with programme strategy. citeturn20view0turn22search2  
- The CoE itself can embed data scientists, pipeline experts, front-end developers, and project managers where needed to accelerate initiatives without replacing domain ownership. citeturn19view0  

**Does Palantir offer certification: yes, but with a specific shape.** Palantir operates a public learning portal where courses are free, and explicitly states that certification exams have a cost and are accessed through a Palantir point of contact or Foundry administrator. citeturn1search1turn1search39 Palantir also publishes certification exam study guides on the same portal. citeturn1search14 Inside Foundry, a Training application surfaces curated learning content and links back to Palantir Learn. citeturn1search32

**Governance cadence: Palantir’s suggested operating rhythm is explicit.** Palantir suggests:
- A quarterly SteerCo for vision, scope, and platform cost decisions. citeturn18view0  
- A monthly development and roadmap review for progress, business-case assessment, and prioritisation decisions. citeturn18view0  
- Daily use-case standups and bi-weekly sprint planning for delivery execution. citeturn18view0  
- Ongoing support-team development, escalating from programme-handled support in Phases 1 and 2 to a dedicated internal Foundry support team from Phase 2 onward. citeturn18view0  

**Champion cultivation: Palantir industrialises champion creation via public stages and communities.** Palantir runs major customer conferences like **FoundryCon** (described as a flagship customer conference). citeturn10search1turn10search13 Palantir also runs **AIPCon**, positioned as a recurring customer event. citeturn10search0turn10search8 At AIPCon, Palantir announced the **Palantir Developer Community** as a public forum for users and early adopters to ask questions, make product suggestions, and share knowledge. citeturn10search15turn10search6 Outside Palantir itself, user groups exist (for example, a Boston Palantir User Group). citeturn10search2

**How champions are armed with ROI: Palantir’s own governance templates point to business-case packaging.** The roadmap review explicitly includes business-case assessment for use case value, and SteerCo agendas include review of use-case value and associated costs. citeturn18view0 This means “ROI ammunition” is expected to be produced continuously as part of governance, not retrofitted at renewal time.

## The scale phase and graduation

**What “graduation” is in Palantir’s own words.** In Phase 4 (“Hypergrowth”), Palantir states the Foundry programme should be “largely self-sufficient”; Palantir engineers can be brought in for “use case boosts and other advisory services”, but daily operations and forward-facing building initiatives are managed entirely by internal teams. citeturn18view4turn2search9

**The practical withdrawal mechanism: shift from Palantir-led delivery to customer-run support and governance.** Palantir’s governance processes call for building an internal support structure that can triage and resolve issues, and explicitly notes that from Phase 2 onward Palantir recommends establishing a dedicated internal Foundry Support team. citeturn18view0 Phase 3 role definitions make the intent unambiguous: Support Services teams exist to “build autonomy” and “reduce reliance on Palantir support services”. citeturn19view0

**What replaces embedded FDE presence: a platform-native support and workflow system.** Palantir documents an internal “Issues application” that provides transparency by tracking past questions, prior Palantir Support engagement, and a resolution timeline. citeturn11search12turn11search29 Palantir also documents how customers file support tickets when self-service resources do not resolve an issue. citeturn11search3 This is effectively the “post-FDE” operating channel: structured support workflows plus customer-run first-line support.

**Self-service enablement is not a slogan: it is a designed platform plus a designed programme role.** Palantir’s platform documentation explicitly frames the platform as empowering a “diverse community of builders” through application-building tools, workflow tools, analytics, and developer tools, all sitting on common security and governance primitives. citeturn1search33turn5search9 At the programme layer, Palantir defines an Education and Training Expert role responsible for aligning training to use case prioritisation, integrating Foundry education into HR onboarding and continuing education, and running “train-the-trainer” mechanisms to scale learning beyond a single resource. citeturn20view0

**AIP-specific accelerators for self-service: AI as a “lower the floor” tool.** Palantir documents “AI FDE” as an agent that can translate natural language requests into Foundry operations, including building and maintaining the Ontology. citeturn0search2 Separately, Palantir documents that AIP features are embedded into core Foundry applications to accelerate workflows, with administrators able to govern usage via Control Panel. citeturn5search24

**How long it takes, and the failure modes: the honest answer based on what Palantir discloses.** Palantir does not publish a universal “time to autonomy” metric. What Palantir does disclose is that pilots may fail to convert: it provides platforms at no or low cost for pilots, and there is “no guarantee” it can move customers from Acquire into later phases. citeturn17view2turn16view3 Palantir also discloses that some significant customers have reduced spend or terminated agreements in the past, sometimes requiring refunds, and contracts may not auto-renew. citeturn17view3  
 
## Lock-in, switching costs, and revenue protection

**What makes Palantir hard to leave: the most evidence-backed mechanism is compounding network effects.** Palantir claims its platforms generate network effects by functioning as “application aggregators”. It gives a concrete example: at one financial services customer, network effects enabled scaling from a single use case to **more than 70 workstreams** across compliance, front office, risk, and internal audit, with each new application built on a shared foundation and with reuse and repurposing across use cases. citeturn17view1  
 
**Lock-in is built from five interacting assets that compound.**  
First: Data integration footprint grows because Foundry is designed as a backbone for complex environments and explicitly aims to reduce the cost of data integration over time via force-multiplier capabilities. citeturn5search9turn5search29  
Second: Ontology complexity grows because the Ontology is positioned as the organisation’s operational layer and “digital twin”, mapping datasets and models to object types, link types and action types. citeturn0search9turn1search2  
Third: Application dependency grows because apps and workflows are built on top of that shared ontology foundation, as described in the “application aggregator” example. citeturn17view1  
Fourth: Institutional knowledge and operational state accumulate via user edits, operational workflows, and auditable governance. For example, Foundry objects can be modified by user edits plus datasource updates, requiring conflict resolution strategies to transparently resolve competing values. citeturn5search7turn5search31  
Fifth: Security configuration becomes a moat because permissions, restricted views, markings, and approvals become deeply intertwined with how data is used across domains. citeturn19view0turn22search6turn22search0

**Switching cost estimates: what can be cited responsibly.** Public sources often throw around specific migration cost numbers, but Palantir does not publish a standard “cost to switch” figure in the sources reviewed here. What is defensible is the qualitative position that Foundry’s Ontology and embedded workflows can create meaningful switching costs; even equity research commentary has framed switching costs as linked to Palantir’s Ontology. citeturn4search15  
A practical way to estimate switching cost in a specific client is to model five workstreams: re-platform integrations, rebuild semantic layer, rebuild apps, reimplement governance and security, and retrain teams. That estimate will vary dramatically by number of connected systems and number of mission-critical workflows, which aligns with Palantir’s own emphasis on platform scope and cost being managed at SteerCo level. citeturn18view0

**Has any large customer left: there is evidence of meaningful non-renewals and explicit exits.** The Financial Times reported the UK government ended a Palantir post-Brexit border deal due to “budget pressures” and planned to build a similar system in-house. citeturn12news49 NHS England also published a contract notice describing a 12-month “transition and exit” service from the Palantir Foundry platform to a new Federated Data Platform supplier for critical Covid-era products. citeturn12search6 Separately, Palantir’s filings acknowledge that some customers have reduced spend or terminated agreements and that Palantir has at times refunded amounts. citeturn17view3

**How Palantir responds to churn threats: the clearest public signal is commercial consolidation plus discounts, not “panic discounting”.** The U.S. Army’s 10-year enterprise agreement with Palantir was positioned as consolidating roughly 75 separate arrangements into one contract vehicle, with volume-based discounts and a $10bn ceiling. citeturn14search2turn14search12turn14news44 That is a classic scale-phase move: consolidate purchasing, standardise procurement, and lock in a long time horizon while retaining flexibility to buy specific capabilities.

**Defence against Microsoft Fabric and Databricks encroachment: partner where it reduces displacement risk.** Palantir publicly announced a strategic product partnership with Databricks that combines Palantir’s AIP and Ontology System with Databricks’ data engineering and processing scale, explicitly framed as reducing total cost of ownership and accelerating production AI. citeturn15search0turn15search4 Palantir also describes a strategic partnership with Microsoft focused on enabling faster Foundry deployment on Azure. citeturn15search1turn15search5 This is best interpreted as revenue protection by turning likely competitors into infrastructural complements.

**Premium pricing justification: the argument is “operational software with governance”, not “a lakehouse”.** Palantir’s bootcamp methodology is framed around shipping operational capability fast and building durable feedback loops, not just analytics prototypes. citeturn9view0turn6search1 Foundry is positioned as integrating governance, lineage, and security deeply enough to support mission-critical workflows, with Phase 3 and 4 adoption frameworks explicitly calling out permissions architecture, compliance roles, and ongoing platform maintenance ownership. citeturn18view3turn19view0turn18view4
